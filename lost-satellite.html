Lost Satellite Studios

Eve of Destruction

A 2D action-platformer built around combo combat, unlockable abilities, and branching progression. As Technical Designer on a small team, I designed and implemented the combat, enemy, and movement systems in GDScript. Steam demo available.

Role

Technical Designer

Timeline

2024

Stack

Godot, GDScript

01 - Combat System

Combat

Designed combat to reward timing and decision-making over repeated input. Each attack is part of a finite state machine, with input buffering controlling when the next move can trigger. This gave the combat a clearer sense of structure and commitment, while also making combo flow easier to tune and maintain.

I also built directional combo branches so players could vary attacks through simple inputs rather than complex commands. This let the system offer meaningful choice without becoming difficult to read. In addition, I designed a separate Berserk moveset to shift the pace and feel of combat when the mode activates, while keeping transitions consistent with the core combat logic.

02 - Enemies

Enemies

Crystal Eaters & Stars

Designed core enemies to pressure the player through coordination, spacing, and timing rather than simple aggression. Their behaviour was built to create more interesting combat situations by forcing repositioning and preventing encounters from becoming too static or repetitive.

I implemented these enemies using shared systems for health, damage, and drops, while exposing balance values through external resources. This made iteration faster and allowed design changes to be tested without rewriting underlying logic.

Boss: Sword-and-Shield Knight

I wanted the knight to feel unpredictable on any given attack but learnable over time. It picks between thrusts, overhead strikes, and a branching multi-hit combo using weighted randomization, so the player can't just memorize a fixed pattern. After every three committed attacks it drops into a recovery window, which gives the fight a rhythm the player can exploit once they recognize it.

I also added directional shield logic so positioning became part of the fight, rather than damage output alone. This helped make the encounter feel more tactical while staying consistent with the game’s broader combat language.

03 - Movement & Traversal

Movement

The dash was the trickiest part of the movement system to get right. It needed to work as both a combat dodge and a traversal tool, and those two uses pull in opposite directions. Too fast and enemies become trivial, too much endlag and platforming feels sluggish.
