Coffin-Likker

A 2D top-down stealth game where you can only see what your flashlight reveals. Everything outside the beam is pitch black. Enemies exist whether you can see them or not. The entire game is designed around that asymmetry and the fear it creates.

Vision system screenshot

Vision & Visibility

The core mechanic flips the usual stealth formula. Instead of hiding in darkness, the player carries a flashlight that determines what's visible. Custom HLSL shaders repurpose URP's Blend Style 3 as a visibility mask. Anything outside the light is truly invisible (zero alpha, not fog or overlay).

There's a deliberate gap between what you can see and what can see you. Shadows appear at the edge of your light before you can make out the object casting them. You might spot an enemy at the far reach of your beam, but it won't react until you get closer. This grace zone gives the player a split second to decide without breaking the tension.

The art direction supports this: black, grey, white, and red only. When the game is about what you can and can't see, colour is noise. Red means danger, nothing else.

Enemy AI screenshot

Enemy AI System

Four enemy types, each built with Unity Behavior Graph and custom C# action nodes: a standard patrol unit, a Weeping Angel that freezes when lit, an aquatic enemy that lurks underwater and surfaces to attack, and a ranged variant with layer-filtered projectiles.

Enemy detection is independent from player visibility. An enemy can detect the player even when the player can't see it. The EnemyManager checks an array of modular detection components (vision cones, proximity, line-of-sight) at a configurable interval, so new detection types slot in without touching existing code.

Combat & Sound Propagation

Firing a weapon spawns a projectile and a sound ring. The ring alerts nearby enemies using Physics2D.OverlapCircleAll, so every shot is a tactical decision that reveals your position. Per-enemy sensory toggles (deaf, blind) let level design create enemies that only respond to specific stimuli.

Water surface effects, procedural reed systems, and environmental interactions are in active development.

Designed the gameplay mechanic, coded it, and collaborated with an artist on visual design. Built in a two-week game jam using Godot.

Calm Before the Storm

A small game about catching fish and reaching an island before a storm hits. The storm arrives at a random time, the boat handles poorly on purpose, and the music builds tension as you play. The whole design is built around that feeling of uncertain pressure.

Led visual design and built the game in a one-week jam using Godot.
